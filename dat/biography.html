Artificial intelligence is increasingly incorporated in high stakes workflows, but AI adoption comes with hidden data management and systems costs that can stifle teams without deep engineering support. My research focuses on building tools to help teams understand and manage the data behind their AI, and broader data context. I take a human-centered approach to this problem, using qualitative methods to understand how teams work with AI in practice, and co-designing systems that fit into their workflows and support their needs.

My Ph.D. work at UC Berkeley, advised by Joe Hellerstein in the RISE Lab, produced FlorDB, a logging and checkpointing database that lets developers query the context of past machine learning runs, as dataframes, even for things they didn't think to log at the time (VLDB, CIDR). To get better insight into MLOps processes and workflows, I co-led an interview study with Shreya Shankar of ML engineers across industries (CSCW). We found that production ML is far more manual, iterative, and team dependent than we may have originally assumed. Engineers can't always answer questions about what their models did and why, and they manage through constant monitoring as well as workarounds like on-call rotations, handwritten filters, and hard won institutional knowledge. I am a fortunate recipient of an NSF Graduate Research Fellowship, and a UC Berkeley Chancellor's Fellowship, which supported my Ph.D. work.

I'm currently a postdoc at Arizona State University's VADER Lab, advised by Ross Maciejewski. At ASU, I've been putting these ideas to work in AI and Law, submitting three NSF proposals as Co-PI, and collaborating with faculty at the Sandra Day O'Connor College of Law on AI-assisted review of public case files. I'm also working with Ransalu Senanayake in Databases and Robotics, leading an in situ contextual inquiry of logging practices in robotics research, and bringing data management to physical world experiments where scenarios without viable replay are common.
