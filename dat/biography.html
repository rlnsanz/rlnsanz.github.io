AI systems are increasingly embedded in high-stakes workflows, but adopting them comes with hidden data management and systems costs that trip up teams without deep engineering support. I believe that shouldn't be the bottleneck, that domain experts like lawyers, roboticists, and scientists should be able to integrate AI into their work without losing visibility into what it's doing or control over how it evolves. I build tools to make that possible.

My Ph.D. work at UC Berkeley, advised by Joe Hellerstein in the RISE Lab, produced FlorDB, a logging and checkpointing database that lets developers query the context of past ML runs, even for things they didn't think to log at the time (VLDB, CIDR). To understand the problem more broadly, I co-led an interview study with Shreya Shankar of ML engineers across industries (CSCW). We found that production ML is far more manual, iterative, and team-dependent than the tooling assumes. Engineers can't always answer questions about what their models did and why, and they manage through workarounds like on-call rotations, hand-written filters, and hard-won institutional knowledge. That gap between how teams want to work with AI and how they actually can is what my research targets.

I'm currently a postdoc at Arizona State University's VADER Lab, advised by Ross Maciejewski. At ASU, I've been putting these ideas to work in AI and Law, collaborating with faculty at the Sandra Day O'Connor College on AI-assisted review of public case files; and Ransalu Senanayake in Databases and Robotics, bringing data management to physical world experiments where scenarios without viable replay are common.
