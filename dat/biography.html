Artificial intelligence is increasingly incorporated in the work of legal teams, research labs, and newsrooms, where conclusions must be defensible against later review, but AI adoption comes with hidden data management and systems costs that can stifle teams without deep engineering support. My research builds tools to help teams understand and manage the data that powers their AI systems. I use qualitative methods to study how teams work with (physical) AI in practice, and co-design systems that fit their workflows.

My Ph.D. work at UC Berkeley, advised by Joe Hellerstein in the RISE Lab, produced FlorDB, a logging and checkpointing database that lets developers query the context of past machine learning runs, as dataframes, even for things they didn't think to log at the time (VLDB '21, CIDR '25). To get better insight into MLOps processes and workflows, I co-led an interview study with Shreya Shankar of ML engineers across industries (CSCW '24). We found that production ML is far more manual, iterative, and team dependent than we may have originally assumed. Engineers can't always answer questions about what their models did and why, and they manage through constant monitoring as well as workarounds like on-call rotations, handwritten filters, and hard won institutional knowledge. I am a fortunate recipient of an NSF Graduate Research Fellowship, and a UC Berkeley Chancellor's Fellowship, which supported my Ph.D. work.

I'm currently a postdoc at Arizona State University's VADER Lab, advised by Ross Maciejewski. At ASU, I've been putting these ideas to work in AI and Law, submitting three NSF proposals as Co-PI, and collaborating with Tyson Winarski at the Sandra Day O'Connor College of Law on AI-assisted review of texts and documents. I'm also working with Ransalu Senanayake, leading an in situ contextual inquiry of logging practices in robotics labs, as part of my broader research in data management for physical world experiments (where scenarios without viable replay are common).
